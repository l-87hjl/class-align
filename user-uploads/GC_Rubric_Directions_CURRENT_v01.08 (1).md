# Core Principle

When creating Google Classroom rubrics for this teacher, build the rubric around **standards-matched categories**, not around random assignment parts.

Google Classroom can match rubric categories to tagged learning goals, so each important tagged standard should usually become its own rubric criterion.

The teacher prefers a **hybrid standards-based rubric**:

- Specific standards each get their own 0-4 criterion.  
- Remaining completion, effort, organization, required elements, and overall quality go into one larger holistic category.  
- The rubric can total any number of points, such as 20, 50, 100, 200, 300, or more.  
- The standards-based criteria guide skill assessment and allow Google Classroom standard matching.  
- The holistic category preserves grading flexibility and equity.  
- The number of standards should be determined by the assignment, not by a default pattern. Do **not** default to four standards just because many examples use four.  
- Examples in rubric directions are examples only. Use language such as **"including but not limited to," "such as,"** or **"for example"** when listing assignment elements so examples are not treated as literal, exhaustive requirements.

The purpose is not to create a long checklist. The purpose is to make Google Classroom standards matching meaningful while keeping grading flexible and fair.

---

# Grading Philosophy

The rubric should not punish students for every tiny missing detail if the overall work shows understanding.

This teacher wants:

- standards-based categories to guide objective skill assessment;  
- completion and following-directions requirements captured somewhere;  
- a larger holistic category for overall quality, effort, coherence, completion, and grading equity;  
- proficiency to mean successful, not almost successful.

Important principle:

**3 \= Proficient / meets the standard.**  
**4 \= Advanced / exceeds the standard.**

Do not treat 4 as the only good score. A student who earns 3 on a standards category has met that standard.

# Standards-Based 0-4 Rubric Framework

Use this general framework for standards-based categories unless the assignment requires more specific language.

## 4 \- Advanced / Exceeds Standard

The student exceeds the standard with strong, precise, and well-developed understanding. Work may show depth, insight, independent application, strong development, meaningful connections, or especially purposeful choices.

## 3 \- Proficient / Meets Standard

The student clearly meets the standard. Work is complete, accurate, understandable, and appropriate to the task. This is the expected target level for successful student work.

## 2 \- Progressing / Approaching Standard

The student partially meets the standard. Work shows some understanding but may be general, uneven, incomplete, repetitive, underdeveloped, or inconsistent.

## 1 \- Developing / Below Standard

The student shows limited evidence of the standard. Work may be vague, minimal, unclear, inaccurate, incomplete, or weakly connected to the task.

## 0 \- Missing / Not Assessable

The student provides no meaningful evidence of the skill, the work is missing, or the response is too incomplete/off-task to evaluate.

## Progression Summary

- **1 \= Attempting, but unclear or incomplete**  
- **2 \= Partly correct, but general or inconsistent**  
- **3 \= Clear, accurate, and complete**  
- **4 \= Deep, precise, insightful, or especially effective**

Key distinctions:

- **2 vs. 3:** Level 2 is generally correct but lacks clarity, completeness, consistency, or development. Level 3 clearly meets expectations.  
- **3 vs. 4:** Level 3 correctly applies the skill. Level 4 extends thinking with depth, insight, precision, or stronger control.

Important notes:

- Length does not determine level.  
- Completion alone does not indicate proficiency.  
- Strong performance includes clear understanding, accurate application, and meaningful explanation.

# CER / Analytical Writing Note

When a standard category involves claim, evidence, and reasoning, use this interpretation:

- A strong response answers the prompt with a clear claim.  
- Evidence must be relevant, accurate, and connected to the claim.  
- Reasoning must explain how the evidence supports the claim.  
- Advanced reasoning may also explain why the evidence matters or what it reveals.

For reasoning specifically:

- Basic reasoning explains what the evidence shows.  
- Strong reasoning explains how the evidence supports the claim.  
- Advanced reasoning explains why it matters or what it reveals.

A quote alone is not sufficient. Strong analysis focuses on specific details, explains connections clearly, and builds a line of reasoning rather than listing isolated points.

# Required Structure

## 1\. Identify the assignment's strongest standards first

Read the assignment directions carefully and identify the standards that are actually being assessed. Use only standards that connect clearly to required student work. Do not tag standards just because they are generally related to English class.

Do **not** default to a fixed number of standards. Four standards is a common example because many English assignments involve reading/thinking, writing, language, and sometimes presentation skills, but four is not a target, minimum, or cap.

Choose the number of standards by analyzing the assignment's actual required skills:

- Short, narrow, or mostly completion-based work may warrant only 1-3 standards.  
- Typical skill practice or class assignments may warrant several standards when the task clearly requires them.  
- Focus questions, Think questions, Writes, essays, culminating writing projects, multi-step projects, and assignments completed over time may warrant more standards because students are practicing more distinct assessed skills.  
- The maximum practical number is 9 standards because Google Classroom allows 10 imported rubric categories and one category is reserved for Holistic Assignment Performance.

Identify the anchor standard first: the one standard most central to the task. Then add supporting standards only when they are directly demonstrated by required student work.

Guiding questions:

What is the main skill students are practicing or demonstrating?

Which additional standards are directly required by the assignment, not merely related to English class?

Does this standard connect directly to what students are required to do?

Google Classroom allows a maximum of 10 rubric categories/criteria in an imported rubric. Because this system reserves one category for Holistic Assignment Performance, the practical maximum is usually:

- **9 standards-based categories \+ 1 holistic category \= 10 categories total**

If more than 9 standards seem relevant, select the 9 strongest standards that are most directly demonstrated by required student work. Absorb remaining related expectations into the closest matching standard category or into the holistic category.

Do not create a vague "Standards Alignment" category if Google Classroom is already matching standards to rubric categories. Instead, make the actual standards the small rubric categories.

## 2\. When asked to identify standards, include a copy-paste CA CCSS list

When the teacher asks to **ID standards** or **identify CA CCSS standards**, the response may include explanations, rankings, anchor/supporting notes, or optional/weaker standards. However, always end with a clean copy-paste list that the teacher can place directly into the Google Classroom assignment description.

Use this exact final heading and format:

```
CA CCSS:
- RL.9-10.2
- W.9-10.4
- L.9-10.6
```

Rules for this final list:

- Include only the recommended standards, not optional or weaker standards.  
- Use one bullet per standard.  
- Use the standard code only, without long explanations, sub-bullets, or extra labels.  
- Keep the list clean enough to copy into Google Classroom while tagging standards and matching standards to rubric criteria.  
- If a substandard is specifically recommended, include the substandard code, such as `L.9-10.4b`.

## 3\. Make each selected standard its own 0-4 rubric criterion

Each selected standard should receive its own category / criterion. The criterion title should begin with the standard code so Google Classroom and the teacher can easily match it.

Example criterion titles include, but are not limited to:

- RL.9-10.2 \- Theme / Central Idea Development  
- RL.9-10.3 \- Character Development and Roles  
- RL.9-10.4 \- Word Choice, Tone, Figurative Language, and Meaning  
- W.9-10.4 \- Clear and Coherent Written Explanation  
- W.9-10.9 \- Evidence-Based Literary Analysis  
- W.9-10.10 \- Reflection, Completion, and Writing for a Range of Tasks  
- SL.9-10.5 \- Strategic Use of Digital Media / Presentation Design  
- L.9-10.1 \- Grammar and Usage  
- L.9-10.6 \- Academic and Literary Vocabulary

Each standards-based criterion should usually be worth 4 points, with levels:

- 4 \- Advanced  
- 3 \- Proficient  
- 2 \- Progressing  
- 1 \- Developing  
- 0 \- Missing / Not Assessable

## 4\. Connect assignment actions directly to standards

Each standard category should assess the assignment requirement that actually belongs to that standard. Avoid creating disconnected checklist categories if the requirement fits under a standard.

For **RL.9-10.1**, use the category for evidence and inference work, including but not limited to:

- using direct quotes, paraphrases, or specific textual details;  
- making inferences from character actions, diction, imagery, dialogue, events, or structure;  
- explaining what evidence shows or suggests.

For **RL.9-10.2**, use the category for theme or central idea work, including but not limited to:

- explaining what a character's dream, conflict, or choices reveal about larger ideas;  
- connecting a student's reflection or creative choice to a text's larger themes;  
- showing understanding of ideas such as dreams, the American Dream, belonging, justice, fear, responsibility, power, identity, friendship, hope, or growth.

For **RL.9-10.3**, use the category for character work, including but not limited to:

- analyzing complex characters;  
- identifying character traits, motivations, conflicts, and changes;  
- explaining how characters interact, advance plot, or develop theme.

For **RL.9-10.4**, use the category for word choice, tone, figurative language, or literary language, including but not limited to:

- analyzing diction, tone, imagery, sound devices, figurative language, or connotation;  
- explaining how language contributes to meaning, mood, tone, or theme.

For **W.9-10.4**, use the category for clear and coherent written communication, including but not limited to:

- captions, explanations, reflections, short responses, paragraphs, or written labels;  
- organization, clarity, development, completeness, and appropriateness to task, purpose, and audience;  
- writing that helps the reader understand the student's thinking.

For **W.9-10.9**, use the category for evidence-based analysis of literary or informational texts, including but not limited to:

- using evidence to support analysis, reflection, or research;  
- connecting textual details to claims;  
- integrating direct quotes or paraphrased evidence.

For **SL.9-10.5**, use the category for presentation and visual / digital choices, including but not limited to:

- images, symbols, quotes, icons, screenshots, audio/video, layout, text placement, or visual design choices;  
- design choices that support meaning, clarity, or interest;  
- presentation effectiveness, readability, and visual organization when those things affect communication.

Neatness and presentation effectiveness should usually connect to SL.9-10.5 if they affect communication, readability, layout, visual effectiveness, or meaning. If they are only general care, effort, or completion, they can also be captured in the holistic category.

For **L.9-10.1**, use the category for grammar and usage when conventions are being assessed separately.

For **L.9-10.4 / L.9-10.4b / L.9-10.4c**, use the category for determining word meaning, roots/affixes, context clues, and reference materials when those actions are directly required.

For **L.9-10.5**, use the category for figurative language, word relationships, nuance, or literary language.

For **L.9-10.6**, use the category for academic and domain-specific vocabulary, including literary terms and content-area vocabulary.

## 5\. Extract assignment requirements, then sort them into standards or holistic

From the assignment directions, checklist, slide prompts, sentence frames, teacher notes, examples, submission requirements, and embedded directions, identify every meaningful required element.

Then sort each requirement into one of two places:

1. **A standards category** if the requirement clearly demonstrates one of the selected CA CCSS.  
2. **The holistic category** if the requirement is completion-based, procedural, effort-based, or not clearly tied to one standard.

Examples:

| Instruction / Requirement | Usually belongs under |
| :---- | :---- |
| Use textual evidence or MLA citation | W.9-10.9, RL.9-10.1, or another evidence standard |
| Use transitions, paragraph structure, or clear organization | W.9-10.4 |
| Use images, icons, layout, or visual design choices | SL.9-10.5 |
| Use complete sentences and standard grammar | L.9-10.1, if conventions are assessed separately |
| Use academic/literary vocabulary correctly | L.9-10.6 |
| Analyze character development | RL.9-10.3 |
| Analyze theme or central idea | RL.9-10.2 |
| Analyze tone, diction, figurative language, or sound devices | RL.9-10.4 or L.9-10.5 |
| Complete all required activities, boxes, slides, paragraphs, posts, or submissions | Holistic Assignment Performance |
| Follow procedural directions such as deleting arrows, moving an X, attaching required files, or filling in all spaces | Usually holistic, unless the action directly affects communication or a selected standard |

## 6\. Account for multi-part assignments without multiplying categories unnecessarily

For multi-part assignments, do not automatically create separate point buckets or rubric categories for each part.

Usually, identify the standards that apply across the whole assignment and write each standard category broadly enough to cover all relevant parts.

A single standard category may apply across multiple activities or sections. Do not duplicate the same standard just because the assignment has multiple parts.

For example, avoid creating:

- W.9-10.4 \- Activity 1  
- W.9-10.4 \- Activity 2  
- W.9-10.4 \- Activity 3

Instead, create one category:

- W.9-10.4 \- Clear and Coherent Writing Across the Assignment

The holistic category should account for completion, effort, and overall quality across all parts, including whether each required activity or section was meaningfully attempted. The holistic description may name the parts, but should usually avoid rigid sub-point values unless the teacher specifically requests them.

If the parts are truly different in skill or product type, add standard-specific language rather than multiplying categories.

---

## 7\. Put all remaining requirements into one large holistic category

After each selected standard has its own 0-4 category, place the remaining points in one large holistic category.

This category is usually not linked to a CCSS because it is for grading flexibility, completion, effort, following directions, overall quality, and the final product as a whole.

The holistic category should include, but is not limited to:

- completion of required elements;  
- assignment-specific sections, slides, boxes, prompts, captions, posts, images, responses, or artifacts;  
- completion and quality across all parts of a multi-part assignment;  
- following directions that are not directly tied to a specific standard;  
- evidence of effort, care, revision, and follow-through;  
- overall organization, coherence, readability, and usability;  
- whether the final product works as a complete assignment;  
- flexibility for teacher judgment and grading equity.

Do not make every missing box a separate penalty unless the assignment is purely completion-based. Use the standards categories for quality of thinking/skill and the holistic category for overall completion and product quality.

Example principle:

- A student who fills every box but writes generic nonsense should not score high overall.  
- A student who misses one minor box but shows strong understanding should still have room to do well.

## 8\. Calculate holistic points

The holistic category receives all remaining points after standards categories.

Formula:

```
Holistic points = Total assignment points - Total standards-category points
```

Since standards categories are usually 4 points each:

```
Holistic points = Total assignment points - (number of standards x 4)
```

The holistic category should use this percentage spread across its five levels:

- 100%  
- 85%  
- 75%  
- 63%  
- 0%

Calculate these percentages from the value of the holistic category itself, not from the total assignment value.

| Level | Percent of Holistic Points | Purpose |
| :---- | ----: | :---- |
| 4 \- Advanced | 100% | Complete, careful, coherent, and strong overall product |
| 3 \- Proficient | 85% | Meets assignment expectations successfully |
| 2 \- Progressing | 75% | Partially complete or uneven but shows meaningful work |
| 1 \- Developing | 63% | Limited, incomplete, or weakly developed but shows some attempt |
| 0 \- Missing / Not Assessable | 0% | Missing, off-task, or too incomplete to evaluate |

Example: 200-point assignment with 4 standards:

- 4 standards x 4 points \= 16 standards points  
- 200 \- 16 \= 184 holistic points  
- Holistic levels: 184, 156.4, 138, 115.92, 0  
- Round only when needed for Google Classroom import, while preserving the 100 / 85 / 75 / 63 / 0 pattern.

Example: 200-point assignment with 6 standards:

- 6 standards x 4 points \= 24 standards points  
- 200 \- 24 \= 176 holistic points  
- Holistic levels: 176, 149.6, 132, 110.88, 0

## 9\. Standards-completion offset for gradebook use

Because standards are integrated into Google Classroom rubrics, the standards categories may compress grades if interpreted too rigidly. Competency should function around a C / 70%, and students who complete most of the day's work should not be overly penalized by the standards-linking structure.

When appropriate, calculate a separate standards-completion support or extra-credit offset:

```
Standards-completion offset = 2 points x number of tagged standards
```

This is not necessarily part of the rubric file itself. It is a gradebook support strategy used when standards integration depresses grades more than intended.

Example daily Ozymandias sequence:

| Assignment | Standards | Offset |
| :---- | ----: | ----: |
| Vocabulary | 4 | 8 points |
| Skill: Theme | 4 | 8 points |
| Skill: Alliteration, Consonance, and Assonance | 4 | 8 points |
| Focus Questions | 7 | 14 points |
| Choice Board | 6 | 12 points |
| Total | 25 standards | 50 points |

Suggested Aeries comment:

Extra credit offsets standards-rubric grade compression in Google Classroom; reflects completion of most work and basic competency.

## 10\. Do not create extra categories unless needed

Avoid separate categories such as "Captions," "Neatness," "Presentation," "Completion," "Activity 1," "Activity 2," or "Text Connection" unless there is a strong reason.

Usually:

- Literary connection, theme, character, or central idea work belongs under an RL or RI standard.  
- Written explanation, reflection, coherence, and development belong under a W standard.  
- Visual presentation, media, layout, and design choices belong under SL.9-10.5.  
- Grammar and sentence clarity belong under an L standard when conventions are being assessed separately.  
- General completion, effort, organization, following directions, and overall quality belong under the holistic category.  
- Completion of multiple activities or sections usually belongs in the holistic category unless a specific part directly demonstrates a selected standard.

## 12\. Recommended structure examples

The number of standards depends on the assignment. Do not copy the number of standards from an example unless that number is justified by the actual assignment.

Use fewer standards when the task is narrow, short, mostly multiple-choice, mostly completion-based, or does not directly require many distinct skills. Use more standards when the task is longer, higher-stakes, multi-step, completed over time, or clearly requires students to practice and demonstrate more distinct reading, writing, language, research, speaking/listening, or presentation skills.

The total number of rubric categories must stay within Google Classroom's 10-category limit, including the holistic category.

| Assignment Type | Possible Standards Count | Reasoning |
| :---- | ----: | :---- |
| Short skill practice | 2-3 standards, if warranted | A narrow task may not assess enough distinct standards to justify four. |
| Typical class assignment / project | Usually 4 or more standards | A meaningful assignment often involves writing, reading/thinking, language, and presentation or speaking/listening skills. |
| Longer project, essay, portfolio, or assignment completed over time | 5+ standards when clearly assessed | Higher-point or multi-part work may require multiple reading, writing, language, research, speaking/listening, or media standards. |
| Multi-part final, exam, or performance task | Usually 4+ and sometimes 5+ standards | Identify standards across the full set of activities, but do not duplicate the same standard for each activity unless necessary. |
| Mostly completion-based assignment | Fewer standards may be appropriate | Do not force standards that are not directly demonstrated by student work. |

Example 20-point structure with four standards:

| Criterion | Points |
| :---- | ----: |
| Standard 1 \- Assignment-Specific Skill Category | 4 |
| Standard 2 \- Assignment-Specific Skill Category | 4 |
| Standard 3 \- Assignment-Specific Skill Category | 4 |
| Standard 4 \- Assignment-Specific Skill Category | 4 |
| Holistic Assignment Performance | 4 |
| Total | 20 |

Example 200-point structure with four standards:

| Criterion | Points |
| :---- | ----: |
| Standard 1 \- Assignment-Specific Skill Category | 4 |
| Standard 2 \- Assignment-Specific Skill Category | 4 |
| Standard 3 \- Assignment-Specific Skill Category | 4 |
| Standard 4 \- Assignment-Specific Skill Category | 4 |
| Holistic Assignment Performance | 184 |
| Total | 200 |

If only three standards are truly warranted, the holistic category in a 200-point rubric would be 188 points. If five standards are warranted, the holistic category would be 180 points. If seven standards are warranted, the holistic category would be 172 points.

The standard categories stay small 0-4 skill checks, and the holistic category absorbs the remaining points.

# Special Guidance for Creative or Project-Based Assignments

For creative assignments, first extract the concrete requirements, then group them under the relevant standards and holistic category.

Do not turn every required item into a separate rubric category. That creates a checklist rubric and makes Google Classroom matching less useful.

Example: For a character-based social media / Facebook-style assignment, possible task parts might include:

- profile details;  
- friends or relationships;  
- images;  
- likes/interests;  
- status updates;  
- posts in reverse chronological order;  
- character perspective;  
- message exchange;  
- connection to plot, theme, or relationships.

These should usually be grouped under relevant standards such as:

- RL.9-10.3 for character understanding;  
- RL.9-10.1 for evidence/inference;  
- W.9-10.3 or W.9-10.4 for narrative voice or coherent writing;  
- SL.9-10.5 for visual/digital design;  
- L.9-10.1 or L.9-10.6 if grammar or vocabulary is separately assessed;  
- Holistic Assignment Performance for completion, care, and overall product quality.

The exact standards depend on the assignment. Do not use this example as a required list.

The teacher selects/tags the CA CCSS standards first in Google Classroom. Google Classroom shows those standards as tagged learning goals. The rubric categories need to be written so each standard can be manually linked to its matching category.
