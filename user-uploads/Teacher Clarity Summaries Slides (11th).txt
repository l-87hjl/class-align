b. Develop the topic thoroughly by selecting the most significant and relevant facts, extended definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples appropriate to the audience’s knowledge of the topic.

Success Criteria
(General, by Essay Type)

Argumentative Essay
Planning & Thesis
I can write a strong introduction with a clear hook and transition to thesis.
I can write a single, complex thesis statement that takes a clear, debatable position on the prompt, and is the final sentence of the introductory paragraph.
I can distinguish my claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims.
I can create a "thesis map" by identifying the distinct reasons that support my thesis, which will become my topic sentences.
Structure & Evidence
I can structure my essay logically with an introduction, body paragraphs, a dedicated counterargument paragraph, and a conclusion.
I can support my argument with well-organized body paragraphs that follow a clear “C-E-C” (Claim-Evidence-Commentary) or similar structure, and that move beyond simple restatement to explain how and why my evidence proves my topic sentence.
I can synthesize and draw relevant evidence (facts, quotes, data) from at least three credible sources to support my claims, .
I can properly embed quotes and paraphrases (including using signal phrases or attributive tags to establish the source's credibility), and follow with a correct in-text citation.
I can introduce evidence using signal phrases or attributive tags that establish the source's credibility (e.g., "According to a study from Harvard..." or "As noted by historian Dr. Jane Doe...").
I can write a clear conclusion that restates my thesis and wraps up my argument with a call to action or final insight on the topic.
Analysis & Reasoning
I can explain how and why my evidence proves my topic sentence, moving beyond simple restatement (the "E" in C-E-R).
I can connect my analysis in each paragraph back to my main thesis to maintain a cohesive argument.
Argumentation
I can fairly and thoroughly develop my claims and counterclaims with the most relevant evidence.
I can fairly present a strong opposing claim (counterargument) and support it with evidence, then refute the counterargument by explaining how the opposing claim is weak or invalid.
I can make purposeful rhetorical choices (in tone, style, and use of ethos, pathos, logos) to persuade my intended audience.
Conventions & Style
I can maintain a formal style and objective tone.
I can correctly use an appropriate citation style (e.g., MLA, APA) to credit my sources.
I can correctly modify direct quotes using brackets [ ] (for clarity or grammar) and ellipses [...] (for omission).
I can use appropriate transitions (including linking words) to connect my claims, evidence, and reasoning, and to guide the reader smoothly between paragraphs.
I can create a correctly formatted Works Cited page and format my essay (heading, font, margins, spacing) according to MLA style guidelines.


Informative / Explanatory Essay
Planning & Thesis
I can write a clear, objective, and focused thesis statement that states the topic and the main points I will explain.
I can narrow a broad topic to a specific and manageable focus for the scope of the essay.
Structure & Evidence
I can structure my essay logically (e.g., by topic, chronologically, cause/effect) to make complex information easy to understand.
I can follow a "C-E-R" structure where my "Claim" is a topic sentence, "Evidence" is a fact or detail, and "Reasoning" is an objective explanation.
I can select relevant facts, definitions, concrete details, and examples from sources to develop my topic.
Analysis & Reasoning
I can explain what my evidence means and why it is important for understanding the topic (the "E" in C-E-R).
I can analyze and explain the relationships between pieces of information (e.g., how this cause leads to that effect).
I can maintain an objective tone throughout the essay, without inserting my personal opinions or arguments.

Conventions & Style
I can use precise language and domain-specific vocabulary appropriate for the topic.
I can use transitions to clearly connect ideas and show the logical relationships between them (e.g., consequently, for example, in addition).
I can provide a concluding statement that summarizes the main points and reinforces the topic's significance.



Literary Analysis Essay
Planning & Thesis
I can write an interpretive, debatable thesis statement that makes a specific claim about the text's meaning, theme, character, or craft.
I can move beyond a simple plot summary or obvious statement (e.g., "The author uses metaphors") to a true interpretation (e.g., "The author uses metaphors of decay to illustrate the main character's moral collapse").
Structure & Evidence
I can structure my essay logically around the key points of my analysis (not just a summary of the plot).
I can select the best short, powerful direct quotes from the text that provide the strongest support for my claims.
I can follow a "C-E-R" structure where my "Claim" is an interpretive topic sentence and my "Evidence" is a direct quote from the text.


Analysis & Reasoning
I can provide deep analysis (the "E" in C-E-R) that explains how and why my chosen quote proves my topic sentence, moving beyond restatement.
I can analyze how the author's craft (e.g., literary devices, word choice, sentence structure, tone) creates meaning.
I can connect my analysis in each paragraph back to my main thesis to build a cohesive interpretation.
Conventions & Style
I can properly introduce and integrate quotes so they flow smoothly into my own sentences.
I can consistently use the present tense ("literary present") when discussing events and actions within the text.
I can use proper MLA in-text citations, typically (Author Page#).

Narrative Essay
Planning & Purpose
I can choose a specific, focused "small moment" from my life (not a summary of my entire life).
I can establish a clear purpose or "so what" for my story—the central lesson or insight I want the reader to understand.
Structure & Craft
I can organize my story with a clear and engaging beginning (hook), middle (rising action/climax), and end (resolution).
I can use vivid sensory details ("show, don't tell") to paint a picture and immerse the reader in the setting and action.
I can use narrative techniques like pacing (slowing down or speeding up time), dialogue, and description to bring the story to life.
Analysis & Reflection
I can include my internal thoughts and feelings from the time of the event to show my perspective.
I can include a clear moment of reflection (the "so what") that explains why this story matters, what I learned, or how it changed me.

Conventions & Style
I can use a consistent verb tense (usually past tense) to describe the events of the story.
I can use a personal, authentic voice that sounds like me and fits the tone of the story.
I can use transitions (e.g., later that day, suddenly, after that) to clearly show the passage of time and sequence of events.

What this is: This is the other major writing mode. Instead of arguing a point, students are explaining a complex topic, concept, or process. Think research reports, literary analysis essays, or "how-to" guides.
Standards Not Covered: W.11-12.2 (and all its sub-standards, .a-f).
2. Narrative Writing
What this is: This is the creative writing standard. This project doesn't ask students to write a story, either real or imagined. This would be covered by personal essays, short stories, or memoir-style writing.
Standards Not Covered: W.11-12.3 (and all its sub-standards, .a-e).
3. Full-Process Research
What this is: Your Blackfish project is a "closed-universe" assignment where sources are provided. This is an excellent scaffold. A "full-process" research standard requires students to also generate their own inquiry questions and find, vet, and gather their own sources from scratch.
Standards Not Covered: W.11-12.7.
4. Analysis of Foundational U.S. Documents
What this is: This reading standard specifically focuses on analyzing the themes, purposes, and rhetoric of 17th-19th century historical U.S. documents (e.g., The Federalist Papers, foundational speeches, court opinions). Your current project rightly focuses on contemporary public advocacy.
Standards Not Covered: RI.11-12.9.
5. Analysis of Author's Craft (Structure & Vocabulary)
What this is: Your project focuses on analyzing the argument and rhetoric (ethos, pathos, logos). These "missing" standards focus on a different kind of close reading: analyzing how an author structures their text (e.g., compare/contrast, chronological, problem/solution) and how their specific word choices (including figurative and technical language) refine the meaning.
Standards Not Covered: RI.11-12.4 and RI.11-12.5.
