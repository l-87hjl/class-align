The short answer is: You typically have **one main set of Learning Intentions (LIs) and Success Criteria (SCs) for the core lesson/objective of the day.** The warmup, activity, and exit ticket are all *aligned* to that single set.

Here is the organizational hierarchy, from largest to smallest, using your units as an example.

### **2\. The Lesson Level: Learning Intention (The "Daily Goal")**

## **🧩 How Your Daily Activities Fit (Warmup, Exit Ticket)**

You do **not** need a new "set" of LIs/SCs for every single activity. Instead, each activity is a *tool* to build or check the *main* LI for the day.

Here’s how a single lesson flows, all pointing to the **same LI**:

**Lesson LI:** "I can analyze how an author uses a character's actions to develop a theme of 'responsibility'."

1. **Warmup (5-10 min):**  
   * **Purpose:** To *activate* or *build* a skill needed for the main LI.  
   * **Activity:** "Look at this picture of a messy room. Who is responsible for it? What's the *theme* or *message* of the picture?"  
   * **Clarity Check:** This directly prepares students to think about "actions" and "responsibility" before they even open the text.  
2. **Main Lesson/Activity (30-40 min):**  
   * **Purpose:** To *practice and demonstrate* the main LI.  
   * **Activity:** "Read this short story (e.g., about a scientist who creates a robot). With your partner, find two quotes and fill out the graphic organizer, following our **Success Criteria**."  
   * **Clarity Check:** The SCs are posted and are the *explicit instructions* for this task.  
3. **Exit Ticket (5 min):**  
   * **Purpose:** To *assess* if students met the main LI.  
   * **Activity:** "On your sticky note, answer this: Did the scientist in today's story take responsibility for what they created? Use one piece of evidence (an action) to support your 'yes' or 'no'."  
   * **Clarity Check:** This is a *direct* check of the LI ("analyze... character's actions... theme of 'responsibility'"). You can quickly sort the sticky notes into "Got it," "Almost," and "Not yet" piles.

## **How the Concepts Interconnect**

The article explains that these concepts are deeply connected and build upon each other, with success criteria linking directly to learning progressions23.

1. The **Learning Intention** ("What am I learning?") is the ultimate *destination*—the overall goal of the lesson242424.  
2. The **Success Criteria** ("How will I know I've learned it?") are the specific *evidence* that proves a student has reached the destination252525. They are what students "will do to achieve the learning intentions"26.  
3. The **Learning Progression** is the *pathway* to get there27. The article defines it as the "incremental steps" a student takes—starting from prerequisite skills—to meet *each* success criterion28.

In short, the **learning intention** is the goal, the **success criteria** are the checkpoints that prove the goal is met, and the **learning progression** is the step-by-step map that guides the student to each checkpoint29.

There are two levels of "I can" statements, and you need both.

* Level 1: Learning Intentions (Your "Standards-based I Cans")  
  These are the big-picture learning goals for the lesson, derived directly from the standards. They answer the "What" and "Why" am I learning this.  
* Level 2: Success Criteria (Your "Self-assessment I Cans")  
  These are the specific, task-based steps that show how you'll meet the Learning Intention in this specific lesson. They are the "look-fors" that answer "How will I know I've learned it?"

You don't choose one or the other. You use them together. The **Success Criteria** (Level 2\) are the *evidence* that proves the student is meeting the **Learning Intentions** (Level 1).
